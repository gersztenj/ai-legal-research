How these were made

Each paper began with a question and a thesis that I set. Working with Claude, I surveyed the legal and regulatory landscape, structured the argument, drafted and revised the prose, and assembled the citations, tables, and figures — Claude acting as a fast research assistant and writing partner throughout.

I directed the research questions, checked the legal sources, exercised editorial judgment over every claim, and take full responsibility for the content, including any errors. These are working drafts — citations are still being finalized and sources are current to mid-2026 — posted to demonstrate the method, not as final published scholarship.

The Reactive Literature

Measuring How Banking-Law Scholarship Reaches Public Policy, 2000–2025

Does legal scholarship on bank regulation influence policy, or merely comment on it? This study assembles an original corpus of 1,915 banking-law review articles (2000–2025) and links it to the federal policy record using only open, terms-of-service-respecting government data. The answer is stratified: in the aggregate the literature is reactive — scholarly output tracks policy events at a two-to-three-year lag — but a small, nameable vanguard sits upstream of policy, traceable through citation in congressional and judicial documents, recurring testimony, and movement into government office. A companion interactive dashboard and a Bluebook-formatted citation dataset accompany the paper.

The Rise of the National Trust Charter

Digital Assets, the GENIUS Act, and the Reinvention of the Special-Purpose Bank

In December 2025 the OCC approved five crypto-native national trust charters — Circle, Ripple, BitGo, Fidelity, and Paxos. Read alongside the GENIUS Act, this paper argues the approvals are the third act of a decade-long campaign to detach the national bank charter from deposit-taking, running from the fintech charter through the Brooks-era crypto trust banks to the present stablecoin wave. It shows the new entrants are structurally different from, and far smaller than, the banks the trust charter was built for — and proposes a purpose-built “National Payments and Custody Institution” charter in response.

Sovereign Stablecoins

State-Issued Stablecoins, with Wyoming’s FRNT and North Dakota’s Roughrider Coin as Case Studies

Can a U.S. state issue its own fiat-backed stablecoin — and what happens to the dual-banking system if it does? This paper uses two live experiments, Wyoming’s Frontier Stable Token (FRNT) and North Dakota’s Roughrider Coin, as paired case studies in putting sovereign money on public blockchains. It examines the legal authority states are relying on, how their designs differ, and the federal-state tensions that state-issued stablecoins surface as the GENIUS Act reshapes the field around them.

Silence Without Sanction

The Federal Reserve’s Communications Blackout, the Bowman Episode, and the Law of an Unenforced Norm

For about ten days around each policy meeting, Federal Reserve officials go silent — the FOMC’s communications “blackout period.” Yet the rule is not a statute, a regulation, or a securities rule; it is an internal Committee policy, self-written and, as this Note shows, essentially self-unenforced. Using the June 2026 episode in which the Fed’s top bank supervisor, Michelle Bowman, addressed an invitation-only client dinner hosted by a bank she regulates — during the blackout, hours after the decision — as its anchor, the Note traces the blackout’s history, locates its thin legal authority, shows through the Inspector General’s own investigations why it has no teeth, and proposes a calibrated reform that adds defined consequences and a clearer scope without silencing the Fed’s many voices.

More Than the Maximum

Redemption Discretion, Sponsor Support, and Fiduciary Duty in the 2026 Private Credit Run

In the first quarter of 2026, retail investors tried to pull roughly $14 billion out of the non-traded business development companies and interval funds that now sell private credit to individuals — and the funds answered in strikingly different ways. Some, like Blackstone’s BCRED, honored every request by enlarging the offer and injecting sponsor capital; others held a hard 5% line and returned redeemers as little as 14 cents on the dollar; one simply closed its redemption window. This Note shows that the “5% quarterly cap” the industry markets is really a 5% offer with a 7% ceiling — a two-point cushion built symmetrically into both regulatory wrappers — and argues that honoring more than the maximum is best understood not as generosity but as the return of money-market-fund “sponsor support,” carrying the same implicit-guarantee problem. It locates the legal fault line where a contractual gate meets a fiduciary act, and closes with the disclosure and valuation reforms the episode demands before these products migrate into retirement plans.

The Fourth Function

Research, Data, and Analysis as a Power of the Administrative State — the Federal Reserve, the Statistical Agencies, and the SEC and FTC as Case Studies

Administrative law describes the agency in the image of the three branches — it makes rules, adjudicates cases, and enforces the law. But agencies perform a fourth function the field has scarcely theorized: the production of knowledge. They run the national statistical system, model the economy, survey households, and publish research of enormous influence — yet that research is rarely a “rule,” seldom reviewable “final agency action,” and largely beyond the reach of both the Administrative Procedure Act and the Information Quality Act. This Note furnishes the research function with an administrative-law account of its authority, scope, and impact through three contrasting case studies: the Federal Reserve (insulated research, and supervisory stress-test models that operated as de facto capital rules without notice and comment), the Census Bureau and the Bureau of Labor Statistics (statistics with direct legal force — apportionment, and CPI indexing of Social Security, Treasury securities, and the tax brackets), and the SEC and FTC (economic analysis that the courts have made a precondition to valid rulemaking). Its organizing claim is an “accountability paradox”: legal control over a body of agency research tracks its legal operativeness, not its real-world influence — so the most consequential research is frequently the least constrained.

Measuring the Fourth Function

An Empirical Companion: The Scholarly, Judicial, and Regulatory Uptake of Federal-Agency Research

The empirical companion to The Fourth Function tests the premise on which the doctrinal argument rests — that agency research carries large and unevenly distributed downstream effects. Using three free, terms-of-service-respecting public data sources (OpenAlex for scholarly citation, CourtListener for judicial opinions, and the Federal Register for the rulemaking record), it measures how far the research of three agency clusters propagates into each corpus. The findings sharpen the paradox: by cumulative citation the Federal Reserve is the most influential research producer in the federal government — exceeding the Census Bureau, the Bureau of Labor Statistics, the SEC, and the FTC combined — yet its flagship products are almost wholly absent from the courts, where the statistical agencies’ legally operative numbers predominate. The paper specifies, and previews, a lead-lag test of whether agency research leads policy or instead reacts to it. All data and code are reproducible.

Where this fits

The frontier of AI-assisted legal scholarship

Using AI to help write legal scholarship is new, but it isn’t novel — a small, fast-moving group of scholars has been mapping this terrain since 2022. Here is the landscape, and where these papers sit within it.

  • The first demonstrations (2022–23)

    In December 2022, Suffolk Law dean Andrew Perlman posted The Implications of ChatGPT for Legal Services and Society — most of it generated by ChatGPT in about an hour — as a proof of concept. A wave of “can a machine write a law review article?” experiments followed, including his later Generative AI and the Future of Legal Scholarship.

  • Institutions writing the rules (2024–25)

    The Texas A&M Journal of Property Law published a full issue of AI-assisted articles — co-drafted by professors Andrew Torrance and Bill Tomlinson with GPT-4o and Claude — and its editors proposed a five-level taxonomy for disclosing how much AI a paper used. The emerging norm is disclosure, not prohibition.

  • Evidence it’s already widespread (2025)

    Michael Conklin and Christopher Houston’s Measuring the Rapidly Increasing Use of Artificial Intelligence in Legal Scholarship offered the first empirical evidence of a sharp rise in AI’s footprint across law reviews.

  • What the tools can — and can’t — do

    GPT-4 passed the Uniform Bar Exam (Katz, Bommarito, Gao & Arredondo), and large language models now match human coders on some empirical legal tasks (Choi). But they still invent plausible-looking cases and citations (Stanford’s Large Legal Fictions) — which is exactly why every source in these papers is checked by a human.

Where these papers sit

Most of the public examples so far are either demonstrations — papers about AI, often written by AI to prove it can be done — or benchmarks of raw capability. Fewer are ordinary, substantive doctrinal articles that simply use AI as a disciplined research-and-drafting method: real banking-law scholarship, human-directed, with the law verified by a person. That is the niche this collection aims to occupy. Following SSRN’s own AI policy, the work is disclosed, lists no AI co-author, and leaves a human accountable for every claim.
